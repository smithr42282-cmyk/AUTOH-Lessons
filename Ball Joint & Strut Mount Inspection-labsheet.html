Part B — Dial Indicator Ball Joint Test

Purpose of this Test A dial indicator provides a precise, quantified measurement of ball joint movement. Honda specifies maximum allowable play in millimeters. Visual inspection alone cannot confirm whether play is within tolerance — the dial indicator gives you a number to compare against the service limit.

Equipment Required: Dial indicator with magnetic base, floor jack, jack stands, pry bar (smooth end), safety glasses.

iN Search Path

Service Information → [Year / Model] → Steering & Suspension → Front Suspension → Lower Ball Joint → Inspection Procedure

Record the Honda specification for ball joint axial play from iN before starting the test. Enter it in the Spec column of Table 1 below.

Test Procedure — Follow in Order:

  1. Raise and support the vehicle. Allow the front suspension to hang freely (unloaded). Chock the rear wheels.
  2. Mount the dial indicator magnetic base on a solid, fixed point (rotor hat, subframe, or brake caliper bracket). Never mount on a moving component.
  3. Position the indicator probe against the steering knuckle or hub face, perpendicular to the direction of measurement (axial = vertical play).
  4. Zero the dial indicator. Record the zero reading before applying force.
  5. Place a pry bar under the tire (not the control arm). Apply firm upward force while a partner reads the dial indicator.
  6. Record the maximum deflection displayed. Release and verify the indicator returns to zero.
  7. Compare your measurement to the Honda service specification for this vehicle. Record pass or fail.
  8. Repeat for the opposite side if time permits. Document both sides.

Part C — Control Arm & Stabilizer Bar Bushing Inspection

About Suspension Bushings Rubber bushings isolate vibration and allow controlled movement at pivot points. They are pressed or bonded into the control arm or stabilizer bar brackets. Over time, rubber oxidizes, cracks, or tears — allowing the metal sleeve to shift. Deteriorated bushings affect wheel alignment, increase noise, and reduce handling precision.

Inspect all four bushing locations listed below. Use a flashlight and pick tool for visual access. Pry lightly with a blunt tool to check for movement — do not damage the boot or rubber.

Part D — Excessive Body Roll — Root Cause Identification

What Is Body Roll? Body roll is the side-to-side lean of the vehicle body during cornering. A controlled amount is normal — the suspension is designed to flex. Excessive body roll indicates that the suspension's anti-roll capacity has been reduced. This reduces driver confidence, increases load transfer to the outside tires, and worsens cornering grip.
