Brake Fluid Types
Slide 1 of 3 · Introduction
What Is Brake Fluid — and Why Does It Matter?
Brake fluid transmits the force from your foot on the pedal through the hydraulic system to the calipers and wheel cylinders. The wrong fluid — or degraded fluid — can cause brake failure.
Simplified Honda Brake Hydraulic System
Glycol-Based Fluids (DOT 3, 4, 5.1)
Water-soluble, absorb moisture over time (hygroscopic). This lowers the boiling point — which is why fluid service intervals exist. Most Honda vehicles use DOT 3 or DOT 4.
Silicone-Based Fluid (DOT 5)
Not hygroscopic. Purple color. NOT compatible with Honda ABS systems — do not use in PACT vehicles unless explicitly specified. Can cause ABS failure.
⚠ Honda PACT Note

Always check the reservoir cap and Honda Service Manual for the vehicle in your bay before recommending or adding fluid. Mixing fluid types can contaminate the entire system and require a full flush.

Slide 2 of 3 · Core Concept
DOT Ratings Compared
DOT ratings define minimum boiling points and chemical composition — not brand or quality. Higher number ≠ always better for your Honda.
Rating Dry Boiling Pt. Wet Boiling Pt. Base Honda Use?
DOT 3 401°F (205°C) 284°F (140°C) Glycol-ether ✓ Common
DOT 4 446°F (230°C) 311°F (155°C) Glycol-ether / borate ✓ Some models
DOT 5.1 500°F (260°C) 356°F (180°C) Glycol-ether Rare / performance
DOT 5 500°F (260°C) 356°F (180°C) Silicone ✗ Not compatible
Dry vs. Wet Boiling Points by DOT Rating
🌧 Hygroscopic = Problem

DOT 3, 4, and 5.1 absorb moisture from the air over time. Moisture lowers the boiling point — aged fluid can boil under hard braking, creating vapor bubbles and a spongy pedal.

🔁 Service Interval

Honda recommends brake fluid inspection every 12 months and replacement every 3 years or 45,000 miles — whichever comes first. Always verify in the vehicle's maintenance minder.

Slide 3 of 3 · Honda Application
Applying This in the Bay
Knowing DOT ratings is step one. Applying it correctly on a Honda vehicle is the job.
Step 1 — Find the Spec
Check the reservoir cap first — it's stamped with the minimum DOT rating. Then verify in the Honda Service Manual. The cap wins if there's a conflict only if the manual isn't available — always defer to the Service Manual.
Step 2 — Assess Current Fluid
New fluid: clear to light yellow. Degraded fluid: dark brown or black. Contaminated fluid: cloudy, milky, or has visible particles. Any of these = recommend flush, not top-off.
Step 3 — Top Off vs. Flush
Top off only if fluid is correct color, correct type, and level is between MIN and MAX. If level is low and the fluid is dark, a flush is needed — low level may indicate pad wear drawing fluid down, not a leak.
Step 4 — Never Mix Types
DOT 3 and DOT 4 are compatible in an emergency, but mixing degrades both fluids' wet boiling point. DOT 5 is never compatible with glycol-based systems — it will destroy seals and ABS components.
🔧 Honda PACT Reminder

On your lab sheet for this unit, you must identify the DOT spec from the Service Manual — not just the cap — and document your reasoning for a top-off vs. flush recommendation. Citing the spec is part of your grade.

Knowledge Check · 3 Questions
1. A Honda Accord's reservoir cap is stamped "DOT 3." You only have DOT 4 on the shelf. What should you do?
✓ Correct! DOT 4 exceeds DOT 3 minimum specs and is glycol-based — it is an acceptable substitute. DOT 5 is silicone-based and never compatible with glycol systems.
✗ Not quite. DOT 4 is glycol-based and exceeds DOT 3 minimums — it is a safe substitute. DOT 5 (silicone) is never compatible and would damage seals and ABS components.
2. You pull a brake fluid sample that is dark brown and has a slightly burnt smell. The level is at MIN. What is your recommendation?
✓ Correct! Dark brown color and burnt smell are signs of severe moisture contamination and thermal degradation. Topping off only dilutes the problem. A full flush is required.
✗ Dark brown fluid with a burnt smell indicates heavy contamination and a severely degraded wet boiling point — a top-off won't fix this. A full brake fluid flush is the correct service.
3. What does the "wet boiling point" rating tell you about a brake fluid?
✓ Correct! The wet boiling point simulates fluid that has absorbed moisture over time (3.7% by weight, per FMVSS 116). This is the more realistic in-service spec — aged DOT 3 wet boiling point drops to only 284°F.
✗ The wet boiling point represents fluid after absorbing 3.7% moisture — simulating real-world aged fluid. The dry boiling point is for new, sealed fluid. The wet spec is what really matters for in-service safety.
