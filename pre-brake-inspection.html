🔧 Honda PACT — Why This Matters

A pre-inspection documents the vehicle's condition before you work on it. It protects you legally, gives the customer an accurate picture of their system, and prevents you from missing a related issue. Always complete it before any service begins.

Slide 2 of 3 · Core Concept
The Pre-Inspection Sequence
Follow this order every time. Skipping steps leads to missed findings — and missed findings become comebacks.
01
Review the Work Order & Service History
Read the customer concern. Check Honda service history for prior brake work, TSBs, or recalls. Know what you're looking for before you open the hood.
02
Vehicle Walk-Around — Document Pre-Existing Damage
Record any body damage, paint condition, and wheel/tire condition. Use the shop's vehicle condition form. This protects you and the shop.
03
Check Brake Fluid Reservoir
Inspect level (MIN/MAX), color, and clarity. Dark or milky fluid = contamination. Do not remove cap until you've done a visual check through the reservoir wall.
04
Brake Pedal Check — Engine Off, Then On
Press the pedal with engine off — firm resistance expected. Start engine — pedal should drop slightly (booster activating). Spongy, low, or pulsating pedal = flag it.
05
Visual Inspection — All Four Corners
Check pad thickness through the caliper window. Inspect rotor surface for grooving, heat cracks, or rust lip. Check caliper for fluid leaks. Inspect brake lines and hoses for damage.
06
Parking Brake Function Check
Apply the parking brake fully. Verify the indicator lamp illuminates on the dash. Count clicks if cable-operated — excessive travel may indicate adjustment is needed.
07
Retrieve DTCs — Check ABS / VSA Warning Lamps
Connect the Honda scan tool. Pull any stored DTCs and freeze frame data. Note all active and pending codes. Clear codes only when directed by your instructor or the repair procedure.
⚠ Documentation Rule

Every finding must be recorded on your work order using the Three C's: Concern, Cause, Correction. If it isn't written down, it didn't happen.

Slide 3 of 3 · Honda Application
What to Look For — and What It Means
Knowing what to find is half the job. Here's how to interpret what you see at each checkpoint on a Honda vehicle.
🛑 Brake Fluid
  • Clear / light yellow = acceptable
  • Dark brown / black = degraded, flush needed
  • Milky / cloudy = moisture contamination
  • Level low + dark fluid = flush, not top-off
  • Level low + good fluid = possible leak, inspect lines
🔘 Brake Pads
  • Honda minimum pad thickness: ~1.6mm
  • Uneven wear = caliper sliding pin issue
  • Glazed pads = overheating history
  • Inner worn more than outer = stuck piston
  • Outer worn more than inner = stuck slider pin
⭕ Rotors
  • Deep grooves = rotor replacement likely needed
  • Blue heat marks = thermal overload
  • Rust lip on outer edge = normal light surface rust
  • Lateral runout > spec = pulsation complaint
  • Always measure — never guess
🚨 Warning Lamps
  • Red brake lamp = fluid low or parking brake on
  • ABS lamp = wheel speed sensor or module fault
  • VSA lamp = stability control issue
  • All lamps should self-check and go off at startup
  • Any lamp on = retrieve DTCs before proceeding
Rotor Conditions — Pre-Inspection Reference
Knowledge Check · 3 Questions
1. You press the brake pedal with the engine off and it feels firm. You start the engine and the pedal drops slightly. What does this tell you?
✓ Correct! When the engine starts, the vacuum power booster activates and assists pedal application — causing a slight drop. This is expected and confirms the booster is functioning.
✗ When the engine starts, the vacuum booster activates — causing the pedal to drop slightly under your foot. This is normal and confirms the booster is working correctly. A pedal that does NOT drop would indicate a booster problem.
2. During a visual inspection you notice the inner brake pad is worn significantly more than the outer pad. What is the most likely cause?
✓ Correct! The inner pad contacts the rotor directly via the caliper piston. If the piston is stuck or slow to retract, it applies continuous pressure — wearing the inner pad faster than the outer.
✗ Inner pad worn more than outer indicates the caliper piston is stuck or not fully retracting — keeping the inner pad in contact with the rotor. A stuck slider pin typically causes the outer pad to wear faster.
3. What should you do if you find the brake fluid level is low AND the fluid is dark brown during pre-inspection?
✓ Correct! Dark brown fluid is severely degraded — its wet boiling point is dangerously low. Topping off only dilutes the problem. A full flush is required, and the low level may also indicate worn pads drawing fluid down or a leak.
✗ Dark fluid + low level = full flush required. Topping off contaminated fluid doesn't fix the degraded boiling point. Low level also tells you to check pads for wear (fluid level drops as pads wear) and inspect for leaks before adding any fluid.
