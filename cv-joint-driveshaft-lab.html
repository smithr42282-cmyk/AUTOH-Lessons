KC Question 1 of 6

What is the primary reason rollers and roller grooves must be marked before disassembling an inner CV joint?

✅ Correct! Rollers and their specific grooves wear to each other over time. Installing a roller in a different groove — even on the same spider — will cause accelerated wear, vibration, and noise. Always mark before disassembly.
❌ Not quite. The reason is fit: rollers and grooves wear together as a matched set. Swapping positions — even within the same joint — causes uneven contact, vibration, and premature failure.
KC Question 2 of 6

On most Honda FWD vehicles, which CV joint must be removed to access and replace the CV boots?

✅ Correct! On Honda driveshafts, the inner CV joint (inboard) must be disassembled and removed from the shaft so the boots can slide off toward the differential end. The outer joint stays assembled in most procedures.
❌ Not quite. The inner (inboard) CV joint must be removed so the boot can slide off the shaft. The outer joint typically remains assembled. Always verify in the service manual for your specific model.
KC Question 3 of 6

Why is it critical to use only Honda-specified CV joint grease and not a generic grease substitute?

✅ Correct! CV joints operate under extreme pressure and wide temperature ranges. Honda-spec grease has the correct EP additive package, viscosity, and compatibility with the boot material. Substituting generic grease leads to inadequate film strength and bearing failure.
❌ Not quite. The real issue is lubrication chemistry: CV grease is formulated with EP (extreme pressure) additives that handle the high loads and temperature swings inside the joint. Generic grease lacks this formulation and will fail under CV joint conditions.
KC Question 4 of 6

A vehicle comes in with a clicking noise during slow turns. The noise disappears on straight roads. Which component is the most likely cause?

✅ Correct! Clicking during slow turns is the classic symptom of a worn outer (outboard) CV joint. The outer joint operates at its maximum angle during low-speed turns, and worn ball grooves cause the characteristic clicking. Confirm with inspection of boot condition — torn boots accelerate wear.
❌ Not quite. Clicking during slow turns is the textbook symptom of a worn outer CV joint. The outer joint bends to its maximum angle during low-speed turns. Inner joints typically cause vibration at highway speeds under load, not slow-turn clicking.
KC Question 5 of 6

The spindle nut on most Honda vehicles is a single-use fastener. What does this mean for service?

✅ Correct! Honda spindle nuts use a crimped or staked locking feature that deforms permanently when removed. Reinstalling a used spindle nut risks it loosening in service — which can allow the hub to separate from the shaft. Always install a new nut and torque to spec.
❌ Not quite. Single-use means the nut's locking feature (staking or crimping) is destroyed on removal. A used spindle nut cannot reliably lock itself and risks coming loose, causing catastrophic hub separation. Always replace spindle nuts on installation.
KC Question 6 of 6

After installing a new CV boot, a technician does not equalize the air pressure inside the boot before clamping it. What is the likely result?

✅ Correct! The air inside the boot expands when the CV joint heats up. If pressure isn't equalized before clamping, thermal cycling will balloon the boot and stress the clamp seal, leading to premature cracking or clamp failure. Honda procedure specifies squeezing the boot to proper shape and releasing trapped air before installing the final clamp.
❌ Not quite. Trapped air in the boot expands as the joint heats up during operation. This causes the boot to balloon outward, stressing the boot material and clamp seal. Over time it leads to cracking and grease loss — exactly what you were trying to prevent. Always equalize boot pressure before clamping.
