Slide 1 of 3 — Introduction

Electric Power Steering — Overview

Electric Power Steering (EPS) replaces the traditional hydraulic pump with an electric motor mounted on the steering column or rack. The motor provides steering assist on demand, reducing engine load and improving fuel efficiency.

Honda EPS assembly — steering column, gearbox, tie rods, and linkage

EPS Key Components
  • Steering column & shaft
  • Steering joint (U-joint)
  • Steering gearbox (rack & pinion)
  • Tie-rod ends & locknuts
  • Pinion shaft grommet
  • Ball joint boots
Why Regular Inspection?
  • Loose or worn components cause vague steering feel
  • Damaged boots allow grease loss & corrosion
  • Loose gearbox bolts cause clunking & vibration
  • Worn tie-rod ends affect alignment & tire wear
  • Honda requires inspection at every service interval

⚠ Safety First

Steering system faults are a safety-critical concern. Always complete the full inspection checklist — never skip items because a vehicle looks undamaged. A vehicle can fail steering suddenly if a component is overlooked.

Slide 2 of 3 — Pre-Inspection Procedure

Inspection Procedure — Steps 1 & 2

Before inspecting individual components, Honda requires two preliminary checks performed from inside the vehicle with the engine running and EPS active.

Step 1 — Steering Wheel Play (Longitudinal & Lateral)
With the vehicle on level ground and wheels pointing straight ahead, gently push and pull the steering wheel toward and away from you (longitudinal) and side to side (lateral). Listen and feel for any noise or clearance. Normal condition: No noise, no clearance.
Step 2 — Steering Wheel Rotation Check
Slowly rotate the steering wheel through its full range of motion — lock to lock. Feel for noise, binding, unevenness, or dead spots as the wheel turns. Normal condition: Smooth, even movement with no noise or clearance throughout full rotation.

⚠ Honda Tech Note

If any noise or roughness is detected during Steps 1 or 2, do not proceed directly to component inspection. First, document the symptom precisely — whether it occurs at a specific steering angle, under load, or only when stationary — as this helps narrow the root cause.

What Noise May Indicate
  • Clunk on input → loose gearbox mount or joint
  • Creak when stationary → upper strut bearing or column
  • Grinding through rotation → worn steering joint
  • Vibration → loose tie-rod end or boot damage
What Clearance May Indicate
  • Longitudinal play → column shaft wear
  • Lateral play → steering joint or column looseness
  • Rotational dead spot → worn rack or pinion
  • Any clearance = further component inspection required

✓ Knowledge Check — 5 Questions

Question 1

During the Step 1 pre-inspection, you push and pull the steering wheel toward and away from you. What are you checking for?

✅ Correct! Step 1 checks for noise and clearance in both the longitudinal (toward/away) and lateral (side-to-side) directions of the steering wheel.
❌ Not quite. Answer B is correct. Pushing and pulling the wheel in Step 1 checks for longitudinal noise and clearance — one of two directions checked before the component-level inspection.
Question 2

What is the normal condition for the Steering Joint (Location B) during inspection?

✅ Correct! Location B requires all three conditions: no bolt looseness, no joint clearance, and smooth movement. All three must pass — not just one.
❌ Not quite. Answer C is correct. The steering joint inspection covers three items — looseness, clearance, and movement — and all must be normal. Any single failure requires repair.
Question 3

You find grease leaking from the gearbox boot (Location E). What does this indicate and what is required?

✅ Correct! Grease leaking from Location E (gearbox boot) is not a normal condition. The boot must be replaced to prevent contamination and further damage to the rack and pinion.
❌ Not quite. Answer C is correct. The Honda spec lists "no defects" as the normal condition for the gearbox boot. Any grease leak means the boot has failed and must be repaired — not monitored.
Question 4

Which inspection location specifically requires you to check for clearance at the joint — not just looseness?

✅ Correct! Location G (Tie-Rod End Ball Joint) requires checking for clearance at the joint. Even a small amount of play here causes imprecise steering and must be corrected.
❌ Not quite. The answer is C — Location G. The tie-rod end ball joint is specifically inspected for clearance. Location F checks the locknut for looseness, not the ball joint itself.
Question 5

A technician inspects the pinion shaft grommet (Location H) and finds it is cracked and hardened. What should they do?

✅ Correct! The normal condition for the pinion shaft grommet is no deterioration and no damage. A cracked, hardened grommet has failed and must be replaced to prevent moisture and debris from entering the gearbox.
❌ Not quite. Answer D is correct. Location H specifies "no deterioration and no damage" as normal. A cracked grommet is a failed component — it must be replaced, not monitored or patched.
