1 — Caster

Definition

The forward or rearward tilt of the steering axis viewed from the side of the vehicle. Positive caster tilts the top of the steering axis toward the rear of the vehicle.

Effect on Vehicle

Controls directional stability and steering returnability. More positive caster = more straight-line stability. Unequal side-to-side = vehicle pulls to the side with less positive caster.

Typical Specification Range

Degrees & Minutes +1° 00' to +4° 00'
Decimal Degrees +1.0° to +4.0°
Fraction +1° to +4°
Side-to-side max difference 0° 30' (0.5°)

Positive vs. Negative

Positive (+) — Top of steering axis tilts rearward. Standard on most vehicles. Promotes returnability.

Negative (−) — Top of steering axis tilts forward. Reduces stability. Indicates bent/damaged component.

2 — Camber

Definition

The inward or outward tilt of the wheel and tire assembly viewed from the front of the vehicle. Measured in degrees from true vertical.

Effect on Vehicle

Affects tire contact patch and wear. Positive camber causes wear on the outer edge; negative causes inner edge wear. Unequal side-to-side = vehicle pulls toward the more positive side.

Typical Specification Range

Degrees & Minutes −1° 00' to +1° 00'
Decimal Degrees −1.0° to +1.0°
Fraction −1° to +1°
Side-to-side max difference 0° 30' (0.5°)

Positive vs. Negative

Positive (+) — Top of wheel tilts outward away from vehicle centerline.

Negative (−) — Top of wheel tilts inward toward vehicle centerline. Many modern vehicles run slight negative camber by design for better cornering.

Zero camber = wheel perfectly vertical.

3 — Toe  — Most Critical Tire Wear Factor

Definition

The difference in distance between the front and rear of the tires on the same axle, viewed from above. Toe-in = fronts closer together. Toe-out = fronts farther apart.

Why It Is the Most Critical Tire Wear Factor

Even a small toe error causes the tire to scrub sideways with every inch of forward travel. Incorrect toe produces rapid feathered or diagonal wear and is the most common cause of premature tire failure from alignment.

Specification Formats — All Three Used

Degrees & Minutes 0° 00' to 0° 20' toe-in
Decimal Degrees 0° to 0.33° toe-in
Fraction 0 to ¼° toe-in
Millimeters 0–4 mm toe-in

Toe-In vs. Toe-Out

Toe-In (+) — Front of tires point toward each other. Common on rear-wheel-drive vehicles to compensate for rolling resistance thrust.

Toe-Out (−) — Front of tires point away from each other. Common on front-wheel-drive vehicles. Compensates for drivetrain thrust.

Always check OEM spec — RWD and FWD specs often differ in direction.

⚠ Conversion: mm to Degrees

To convert mm toe to degrees: divide mm measurement by the tire rolling radius (in mm) and multiply by 57.3. Example: 2 mm toe on a 295 mm radius tire → (2 ÷ 295) × 57.3 = ≈ 0.39° (≈ 0° 23'). The alignment machine performs this calculation automatically — understand it conceptually.

4 — Thrust Angle

Definition

The direction the rear axle or rear wheels are pointing relative to the vehicle's geometric centerline, viewed from above. It is the bisector of total rear toe — the direction the rear of the vehicle is actually trying to travel.

Effect on Vehicle

An incorrect thrust angle causes the vehicle to "dog track" — the rear of the vehicle is offset from the front. The steering wheel must be held off-center to drive straight. Also causes uneven front toe wear.

Specification

Ideal value 0° 00' (zero)
Typical allowable ±0° 10' to ±0° 15'
Decimal equivalent ±0.16° to ±0.25°
Positive = rear points left of centerline

4-Wheel Alignment Requirement

A true 4-wheel alignment measures and corrects thrust angle. The front toe is set relative to the thrust line, not the geometric centerline, to ensure the steering wheel is centered and the vehicle tracks straight.

On vehicles with non-adjustable rear suspension, thrust angle is checked to confirm rear axle condition — not adjusted. If out of spec, a bent axle or damaged rear suspension is indicated.

5 — Steering Axis Inclination (SAI)

Definition

The inward tilt of the steering axis from true vertical, viewed from the front. Measured between the upper and lower ball joints (or upper strut mount to lower ball joint on a strut system).

Purpose

SAI reduces the scrub radius and provides steering returnability. It is a built-in, non-adjustable angle — it is a characteristic of the suspension geometry. Used primarily as a diagnostic angle to identify bent or damaged suspension components.

Typical Values

Typical range 8° to 15°
Degrees & Minutes example 11° 30' (11.5°)
Side-to-side max difference 0° 30' to 1° 00'

SAI + Included Angle — Diagnostic Use

Included Angle = SAI + Camber

If SAI is correct but camber is off → bent spindle or strut.
If SAI is incorrect → bent lower control arm or knuckle.

SAI cannot be adjusted — only corrected by replacing damaged components.
