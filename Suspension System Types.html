Slide 1 of 3 — Introduction

Dependent vs. Independent Suspension

All vehicle suspension systems fall into one of two fundamental categories: dependent or independent. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of suspension diagnosis.

Figure 1 — Dependent vs. Independent Suspension: In a dependent system, both wheels share a rigid axle. In an independent system, each wheel reacts to road inputs separately.

Dependent (Solid Axle)
  • Both wheels share one rigid axle
  • When one wheel hits a bump, the other is affected
  • Common on rear axles of trucks and older vehicles
  • Simpler, more durable, lower cost
Independent
  • Each wheel moves on its own
  • One wheel's movement doesn't affect the other
  • Used on most modern passenger cars
  • Better ride quality and handling

⚠ Honda Context

Most Honda vehicles use independent suspension on all four corners — front and rear — for maximum ride comfort and handling precision.

Slide 2 of 3 — Independent Systems

MacPherson Strut & Short-Long Arm (SLA)

The two most common independent front suspension designs you will encounter are the MacPherson strut and the Short-Long Arm (SLA), also called double wishbone.

Figure 2 — MacPherson Strut System. The strut acts as both a structural member and a damper. Single lower control arm with one ball joint.

Figure 3 — SLA (Conventional) Suspension. Upper and lower control arms of different lengths keep the wheel upright through suspension travel. Two ball joints per side.

MacPherson Strut
  • Strut = structural member + shock absorber
  • One lower control arm, one ball joint
  • Compact — works well in FWD engine bays
  • Used on most Honda Civic, Accord front ends
Short-Long Arm (SLA)
  • Separate upper + lower control arms
  • Two ball joints per corner
  • Better camber control through full suspension travel
  • Common on performance and rear suspension applications

⚠ Key Difference

MacPherson struts have one ball joint per corner. SLA systems have two (upper and lower). Always identify which type before ordering parts or performing alignment.

Slide 3 of 3 — Additional System Types

Double Wishbone & Torsion Bar Suspension

Two additional suspension configurations you'll encounter in Honda and general automotive service are the double wishbone (a refined SLA variant) and torsion bar systems.

Figure 4 — Double Wishbone (independent). Both upper and lower arms are wishbone-shaped, providing excellent wheel control. Honda uses this design on many of its performance and rear suspension applications.

Figure 5 — Torsion Bar Suspension. The torsion bar (highlighted in red) replaces a coil spring. One end attaches to the control arm; the other is anchored to the frame. Ride height can be adjusted by rotating the bar's anchor point.

Torsion Bar Key Facts: A torsion bar works by twisting along its length to absorb suspension movement. Because the anchor point is adjustable, technicians can correct ride height without replacing springs — a diagnostic advantage. Some Honda trucks and older Isuzu-based platforms used this design.
