Section 1 — Tire Repair Procedures
Puncture inspection · Damage evaluation · Approved repair method

1A. Puncture Inspection

Perform a thorough visual and physical inspection before any repair attempt.

  1. 1
    Park safely; engage parking brake. Raise and support vehicle on jack stands.
  2. 2
    Remove the wheel assembly from the vehicle.
  3. 3
    Visually inspect the full tread surface for foreign objects — nails, screws, glass, wire.
  4. 4
    Listen for escaping air; apply soapy water to suspicious areas to reveal leaks.
  5. 5
    Mark the puncture location with chalk or tire marker before removing the object.
  6. 6
    Measure puncture diameter with probe or calipers.
    Do not remove the object until ready to repair.

1B. Nail Penetration Evaluation

Evaluate nail depth, angle, and location to determine if repair is possible.

  1. 1
    With object still in place, note the angle of penetration relative to the tread surface.
    Within 10° of perpendicular = potentially repairable.
  2. 2
    Assess puncture diameter: 1/4 inch (6 mm) maximum for repair eligibility.
  3. 3
    Carefully remove the object with pliers. Note depth of penetration.
  4. 4
    Probe the hole with a round-tipped reamer to check for internal cord or belt damage.
    Resistance or fraying = non-repairable.
  5. 5
    Record findings on the repair order.

1C. Repairable vs. Non-Repairable Damage

Use this table to make the repair/replace decision. When in doubt — replace the tire.

✓ Repairable
✗ Non-Repairable — Replace Tire
Puncture in tread area only
Any sidewall or shoulder damage
Nail/screw diameter ≤ 1/4 inch (6 mm)
Puncture diameter > 1/4 inch
Damage angle within 10° of perpendicular
Run-flat damage (driven while flat)
No prior plug-only repair at same location
Multiple punctures within 16 inches
No cord or belt damage visible
Exposed belt or cord damage
⚠ Safety Rules — No Exceptions
▶NEVER repair sidewall or shoulder damage — these areas flex under load and any repair will fail.
▶NEVER perform a plug-only repair — plugs alone do not seal the inner liner.
▶NEVER repair a tire driven while flat (run-flat condition).
▶Inform the customer in writing if the tire is non-repairable.

1D. Approved Repair: Plug + Patch Combination

ℹ Industry Standard
•The only ANSI/RMA-approved method for permanent tire repair is the plug + patch (combination) repair.
•Plug-only or patch-only repairs are NOT permanent and should not be used.
•Always follow the repair kit manufacturer's instructions for cure time and chemical use.
  1. 1
    Remove tire from wheel (see Section 2).
  2. 2
    Buff the puncture area on the inner liner with the specified buffing tool.
    1-inch radius around puncture; 3/4 nap finish.
  3. 3
    Apply vulcanizing cement to the buffed area. Allow to dry to tack (approx. 2–4 min).
  4. 4
    Insert plug stem from inside, pulling through puncture with plug-through tool.
  5. 5
    Peel backing from patch and center over puncture on the inner liner.
  6. 6
    Roll patch firmly with stitching roller from center outward to eliminate air pockets.
  7. 7
    Trim plug stem flush with tread using a sharp knife.
    Do not use scissors; a clean cut prevents plug pull-out.
  8. 8
    Remount the tire onto the wheel (see Section 2).
  9. 9
    Inflate to specified pressure; recheck for leaks using soapy water.

Section 2 — Tire Mounting Procedures
Bead breaking · Wheel inspection · Valve stem replacement · Orientation · Shop safety

2A. Bead Breaking

Safely separate the tire bead from the wheel rim before dismounting.

  1. 1
    Deflate the tire completely by removing the valve core.
  2. 2
    Lay the wheel flat on the tire machine turntable or a rubber mat.
  3. 3
    Position the bead breaker blade 1 inch from the rim edge — never on the rim flange.
  4. 4
    Apply even downward pressure to break the bead on both sides.
    Lubricate bead with mounting lubricant before dismounting.
  5. 5
    Repeat on the opposite side of the tire.
⚠ Wheel Damage Prevention
▶Never place the bead breaker on or over the rim flange — causes permanent rim damage.
▶Do not use a pry bar or steel tools directly against an alloy wheel surface.
▶Use plastic or rubber-coated tools when working on alloy wheels.

2B. Wheel Inspection

Inspect every wheel before mounting a new or repaired tire.

  1. 1
    Clean bead seat area with wire brush; remove all rust, corrosion, and old rubber.
  2. 2
    Inspect rim flanges for cracks, bends, or gouges.
    Cracked or severely bent rims must not be reused.
  3. 3
    Check rim runout with a dial indicator if vibration concerns exist.
    Lateral runout limit: 0.030″ TIR; radial runout limit: 0.045″ TIR.
  4. 4
    Inspect center bore and lug holes for cracks or elongation.
  5. 5
    Document any damage on the repair order and inform the customer before proceeding.

2C. Valve Stem Replacement

Replace the valve stem every time a tire is dismounted. Valve stems are a one-time-use component.

  1. 1
    Cut and remove the old valve stem completely.
  2. 2
    Select the correct replacement stem: snap-in rubber or metal clamp-in per wheel type.
  3. 3
    Snap-in stems: lubricate with rubber lubricant; pull through valve hole with valve stem tool.
  4. 4
    Metal clamp-in stems: seat stem, install washer and nut; torque to manufacturer spec.
    Typical torque: 35–50 in-lb. Over-torquing can crack the valve hole.
  5. 5
    Verify stem is fully seated and perpendicular to the wheel surface.

2D. Proper Mounting Orientation

Always observe directional and rotation markings before mounting.

  1. 1
    Identify the tire's rotation direction arrow on the sidewall if present.
    Directional tires MUST be mounted with the arrow pointing in the correct direction of travel.
  2. 2
    Check for asymmetric tread pattern (OUTSIDE / INSIDE markings on sidewall).
  3. 3
    Align the tire's red dot (radial force variation) with the valve stem if no yellow dot is present.
    If yellow dot (light point) is present, align it with the valve stem.
  4. 4
    Lubricate both tire beads with mounting lubricant. Never use petroleum-based grease.
  5. 5
    Mount the first bead by hand over the rim; use the tire machine to seat the second bead.
  6. 6
    Verify bead is fully seated on both sides before inflating.

2E. Shop Safety During Mounting

ℹ Tire Machine Operation
•Read the machine manufacturer's operating manual before first use.
•Keep hands and fingers clear of the mounting/demounting tool and the rotating turntable.
•Never exceed 40 psi during initial seating inflation — overinflation can cause the bead to explode off the rim.
•Do not stand beside or in front of the wheel while inflating.
⚠ Inflation Cage Required — When to Use
▶Always use an inflation cage (safety cage) for initial bead seating after mounting.
▶Any time a tire has been dismounted and remounted, use the cage.
▶Tires with damaged beads, aged rubber, or unknown service history must be inflated in a cage.
▶Failure to use an inflation cage risks severe injury from bead explosion.

Section 3 — Wheel Balancing
Static & dynamic imbalance · Shimmy diagnosis · Dynamic balancing procedure

3A. Types of Imbalance

Understanding imbalance types is critical for accurate diagnosis and correction.

Type Cause Symptom
Static Imbalance Weight concentrated at one point on the circumference Vertical bounce (hop) at low-to-mid speed
Dynamic Imbalance Weight distributed unevenly across tire width Side-to-side shimmy; often felt in the steering wheel
Shimmy vs. Suspension Vibration Shimmy: tire/wheel out of balance; Vibration: worn or damaged suspension parts Diagnosis required — always balance before replacing suspension parts

3B. Dynamic Wheel Balancing Procedure

Students will perform a complete dynamic balance on each wheel/tire assembly.

  1. 1
    Clean all old wheel weights from the rim using a trim tool. Remove adhesive tape residue.
    Leftover weights will throw off the balancer reading.
  2. 2
    Mount the wheel/tire assembly on the balancer shaft. Seat the cone correctly to center the wheel.
  3. 3
    Enter wheel dimensions into the balancer: diameter, width, and offset.
    Verify with wheel spec chart or measure with the built-in gauge.
  4. 4
    Lower the wheel guard and press START. Allow balancer to complete its spin cycle.
  5. 5
    Read the display: note weight amount and position for both inner and outer planes.
  6. 6
    Rotate the wheel to the 12 o'clock position as indicated by the balancer for each plane.
  7. 7
    Clip-on weights: seat the weight on the rim flange with a weight hammer.
  8. 8
    Adhesive weights (alloy wheels): clean surface with isopropyl alcohol; press firmly and hold 30 seconds.
  9. 9
    Spin the wheel again to verify balance.
    Acceptable residual imbalance: ≤ 0.25 oz (7 g) per plane.
  10. 10
    If out of tolerance, adjust weight placement and re-spin. Repeat until within spec.
✓ Rebalance Verification Checklist
•Final reading shows ≤ 0.25 oz (7 g) residual imbalance on each plane.
•Adhesive weights are firmly bonded — no movement when pressed by hand.
•Clip-on weights are fully seated — no rocking or gaps under the clip.
•Wheel rotation direction verified if directional tire.
•Torque wheel to vehicle specification after installation.
★ Weight Placement Notes
•Static imbalance: single weight on the heavy side at 180° from the imbalance point.
•Dynamic imbalance: weights placed on inner and outer planes at opposing positions.
•Never stack more than two weights in the same location — stacking causes instability.
•Match the weight material to the rim (steel clip-on for steel; adhesive for alloy).
