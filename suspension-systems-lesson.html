Slide 1 of 6 — MacPherson Strut

MacPherson Strut Suspension

The MacPherson strut is the most widely used front suspension design on modern passenger vehicles, including many Honda models. It combines the shock absorber and coil spring into a single compact unit called a strut assembly.

Image placeholder — add your diagram here

Key Components
  • Strut assembly (damper + spring)
  • Upper strut mount & bearing
  • Steering knuckle
  • Lower control arm with ball joint
  • Stabilizer bar (sway bar)
Honda Applications
  • Civic, Accord, CR-V (front)
  • Simple, lightweight design
  • Allows wheel to steer
  • Compact — ideal for FWD engines
  • Easy to service

⚠ Honda Tech Tip

When replacing a strut on a Honda, always use a spring compressor before removing the strut nut. The spring is under high tension and can cause serious injury if released suddenly.

Slide 2 of 6 — Modified Strut

Modified Strut Suspension

The modified strut (also called a strut with lower lateral link) is similar to a MacPherson strut but adds a second lower lateral link for improved geometry control. It provides better camber stiffness under cornering loads.

Image placeholder — add your diagram here

How It Differs from MacPherson
  • Adds a second lower lateral link
  • Better controls wheel camber under load
  • Separates lateral & longitudinal forces
  • More complex than standard MacPherson
Honda / Acura Use
  • Found on Acura TL, TSX rear suspension
  • Some Honda Pilot configurations
  • Improves handling vs. standard strut
  • More bushings = more wear points
Compare to MacPherson: The standard MacPherson uses one lower control arm. The modified strut splits that into two links, allowing each to handle a specific direction of force — lateral (side) vs. longitudinal (front/back).
Slide 3 of 6 — Modified Double Wishbone

Modified Double Wishbone Suspension

The double wishbone (also called double A-arm) uses two wishbone-shaped control arms — one upper and one lower — to locate the wheel. Honda's modified version relocates the upper arm inboard to improve space and geometry.

Image placeholder — add your diagram here

Advantages
  • Excellent camber control through suspension travel
  • Better handling & cornering stability
  • Each arm independently tunable
  • Used on performance & sporty Hondas
Honda Applications
  • Honda Civic Si, Accord (rear)
  • Honda S2000 (front & rear)
  • Acura NSX, Integra
  • "Modified" = inboard upper mount for packaging

⚠ Key Difference

On Honda's modified double wishbone, the upper arm pivot is moved inboard toward the engine bay. This frees up space for a wider engine while maintaining excellent suspension geometry.

Slide 4 of 6 — Short/Long Arm (SLA)

Short/Long Arm (SLA) Suspension

SLA suspension uses two unequal-length control arms — a shorter upper arm and a longer lower arm. This intentional length difference creates a slight negative camber gain as the wheel rises, keeping the tire more upright under cornering.

Image placeholder — add your diagram here

Why unequal lengths? As the wheel rises (bump), the shorter upper arm moves through a greater arc. This pulls the top of the wheel inward, creating negative camber — keeping the tire contact patch flat for better grip during cornering.
Advantages
  • Controlled camber change in bumps
  • Better tire contact during cornering
  • Tunable via arm length ratio
  • Common on rear-wheel-drive vehicles
Disadvantages
  • More parts = more cost & weight
  • Requires precise alignment
  • More wear points than MacPherson
  • Wider packaging space needed
Slide 5 of 6 — Solid Axle

Solid Axle (Dependent) Suspension

A solid axle connects both wheels on the same axle beam. When one wheel hits a bump, both wheels are affected. This is called a dependent suspension because the two wheels are not independent of each other.

Image placeholder — add your diagram here

Where It's Used
  • Trucks & SUVs (rear axle)
  • Heavy-duty & off-road vehicles
  • Older RWD vehicles
  • Honda Ridgeline (IRS used instead)
Pros & Cons
  • ✓ Strong, simple, durable
  • ✓ High load-carrying capacity
  • ✗ Poor ride quality on bumps
  • ✗ Heavy unsprung weight

⚠ Key Term

Unsprung weight — the weight of suspension components below the spring (axle, wheels, brakes). Solid axles have high unsprung weight, which hurts handling because the heavy axle takes longer to follow road irregularities.

Slide 6 of 6 — Torsion Bar

Torsion Bar Suspension

A torsion bar replaces the coil spring with a long steel bar that twists (torsion) to absorb bumps. One end anchors to the frame; the other attaches to the control arm. As the wheel rises, the bar twists — storing and releasing energy like a spring.

Image placeholder — add your diagram here

Key Features
  • Steel bar twists instead of coil spring compressing
  • Ride height is adjustable via anchor bolt
  • Separate shock absorber required
  • Lightweight, compact design
Vehicle Use
  • Honda / Acura SUVs (some front suspension)
  • Mitsubishi, GM trucks historically
  • Popular in off-road applications
  • Easy ride height adjustment for load

⚠ Service Note

Torsion bar ride height is adjusted by turning an anchor bolt at the rear of the bar. Raising the bolt increases pre-load (raises the vehicle); lowering it reduces pre-load. Always check alignment after adjusting ride height.

✓ Knowledge Check — 6 Questions

Question 1

Which suspension type combines the shock absorber and coil spring into a single unit called a strut assembly?

✅ Correct! The MacPherson strut integrates the damper and spring into one strut assembly. It's the most common front suspension on Honda vehicles.
❌ Not quite. The correct answer is C — MacPherson Strut. It's the design that packages the spring and shock absorber into one compact unit attached to the steering knuckle.
Question 2

The modified strut differs from a standard MacPherson strut by adding which component?

✅ Correct! The modified strut adds a second lower lateral link to improve camber control and separate lateral from longitudinal forces.
❌ Not quite. The answer is B. A second lower lateral link is added, providing better geometry control compared to the standard single lower arm MacPherson design.
Question 3

On an SLA suspension, why is the upper control arm intentionally shorter than the lower control arm?

✅ Correct! The shorter upper arm sweeps through a greater arc, pulling the wheel top inward as it rises. This negative camber keeps the tire contact patch flat during cornering.
❌ Not quite. Answer C is correct. The geometry of unequal arms produces negative camber gain during bump travel — this is the whole point of SLA design and improves cornering stability.
Question 4

A solid axle suspension is described as "dependent." What does this mean?

✅ Correct! "Dependent" means the two wheels share a rigid axle beam. When one hits a bump, the axle rotates and the other wheel is forced to move too.
❌ Not quite. Answer B is correct. On a solid axle, both wheels are rigidly connected — movement from one side is transferred directly to the other through the beam.
Question 5

A torsion bar performs the same function as which component in a conventional suspension?

✅ Correct! The torsion bar acts as the spring element — it twists to store energy when the wheel rises and releases it on rebound, just like a coil spring compresses and extends.
❌ Not quite. Answer C is correct. The torsion bar is the spring — it replaces the coil spring. A separate shock absorber is still needed to dampen oscillations.
Question 6

Which suspension system is most commonly found on the front axle of modern Honda FWD vehicles due to its compact, lightweight design?

✅ Correct! MacPherson struts are standard on most Honda FWD models (Civic, Accord, CR-V). Their compact design works well in tight engine bays and keeps weight low.
❌ Not quite. Answer D — MacPherson strut is correct. It's the dominant front suspension design for Honda FWD vehicles because of its compact packaging and simplicity.
